Peppers, scientifically Capsicum spp., are known for their varied flavors and vibrant colors. Thriving in warm climates, peppers come in various shapes and heat levels, such as bell peppers and chili peppers.

- Choose pepper varieties suited to your climate and preferred taste preferences (e.g., sweet peppers, hot peppers).
- Popular sweet pepper varieties include Bell peppers, Banana peppers, and Pimiento peppers. Popular hot pepper varieties include Jalapeño, Habanero, and Cayenne.

- Peppers are typically grown from seeds.
- Start pepper seeds indoors 8-10 weeks before the last frost date in your area.
- Plant seeds ¼ inch deep in seed-starting trays or small pots filled with seed-starting mix.
- Maintain a warm temperature of around 70-80°F for optimal germination.
- Keep the soil consistently moist but not waterlogged.
- Pepper seeds usually germinate within 7-14 days.

- Peppers prefer well-drained, loamy soil with a pH between 6.0 and 6.8.
- Water pepper plants regularly, aiming to keep the soil consistently moist but not waterlogged.
- Mulch around pepper plants to help retain soil moisture and suppress weeds.
- Avoid overhead watering to prevent fungal diseases.

- Peppers have moderate fertilizer requirements.
- Incorporate compost or well-aged manure into the soil before planting to provide nutrients.
- Side-dress pepper plants with a balanced fertilizer (such as 10-10-10) once or twice during the growing season.
- Avoid high-nitrogen fertilizers, as they can promote excessive leafy growth at the expense of fruit production.

- Peppers are typically ready for harvest 60-90 days after transplanting, depending on the variety.
- Harvest peppers when they reach the desired size and color. Most varieties can be harvested when they are firm and fully colored.
- Use a sharp knife or scissors to cut peppers from the plant, leaving a short stem attached.
- Store harvested peppers in a cool, dry place or refrigerate them for longer shelf life.
