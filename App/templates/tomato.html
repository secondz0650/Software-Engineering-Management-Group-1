Tomatoes, scientifically Solanum lycopersicum, are
prized for their juicy texture and tangy flavor.
Flourishing in sunny climates, tomatoes come in
different shapes, sizes, and colors, including red, yellow,
and orange. Packed with vitamins and minerals.

- Choose tomato varieties suited to your climate, garden space, and taste preferences. Options include slicing tomatoes, cherry tomatoes, heirloom varieties, and determinate or indeterminate types.
- Consider factors such as disease resistance, flavor, and size.

- Provide ample light for tomato seedlings. Place them near a sunny window or use grow lights for 12-16 hours per day.
- Keep the soil evenly moist but not waterlogged. Water seedlings when the top inch of soil feels dry to the touch.
- Once seedlings have developed several sets of true leaves, transplant them into larger pots to allow for further growth.

- Transplant tomato seedlings outdoors after the last expected frost date in your area and when soil temperatures have warmed to at least 60°F.
- Choose a sunny location with well-drained soil.
- Space tomato plants 18-36 inches apart in rows spaced 3-4 feet apart.
- Plant seedlings deep, burying the stem up to the first set of leaves, to encourage strong root

- Tomatoes prefer well-drained, fertile soil with a pH between 6.0 and 6.8.
- Water tomato plants regularly, aiming to keep the soil consistently moist but not waterlogged.
- Mulch around tomato plants to help retain soil moisture, suppress weeds, and maintain even soil temperatures. - Stake or cage tomato plants to provide support and keep fruits off the ground

- Incorporate compost or well-aged manure into the soil before planting to provide nutrients.
- Side-dress tomato plants with a balanced fertilizer (such as 10-10-10) once or twice during the growing season.
- Avoid excessive nitrogen fertilization, as it can promote excessive foliage growth at the expense of fruit production.

- Tomatoes are typically ready for harvest 60-80 days after transplanting, depending on the variety. br - Harvest tomatoes when they reach the desired color and firmness. Most varieties can be harvested when they are fully colored and slightly soft to the touch.
- Use a sharp knife or scissors to cut tomatoes from the plant, leaving a short stem attached.
- Store harvested tomatoes at room temperature and out of direct sunlight for best flavor and shelf life.
