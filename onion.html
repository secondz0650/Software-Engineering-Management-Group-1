Onions, scientifically Allium cepa, are valued for their pungent flavor and culinary versatility. Thriving in various climates, onions come in different colors and intensities, such as red, yellow, and white. Rich in antioxidants and nutrients.

- Choose onion varieties suited to your region and intended use (e.g., storage onions, sweet onions, green onions).
- Popular varieties include Yellow Spanish, Texas Sweet, Walla Walla, and Red Creole.

- Onions can be grown from seeds, sets (small bulbs), or transplants.
- If starting from seeds, sow them indoors 8-10 weeks before the last frost date in your area.
- Plant seeds ¼ inch deep in seed-starting trays or pots filled with seed-starting mix.
- Keep the soil consistently moist and maintain temperatures around 70-75°F for optimal germination.
- Onion seeds typically germinate within 7-10 days.

- Once seedlings emerge, provide ample light to prevent them from becoming leggy. A sunny windowsill or grow lights work well.
- Keep the soil evenly moist but not waterlogged. Water seedlings when the top inch of soil feels dry to the touch.
- Thin out overcrowded seedlings to ensure proper spacing, leaving the strongest ones to grow.
- Transplant onion seedlings outdoors once they have developed several sets of true leaves and the soil has warmed up.
- Choose a location with full sun and well-drained soil.
- Plant seedlings 1 inch deep and space them 4-6 inches apart in rows spaced 12-18 inches apart.
- Press the soil gently around the roots and water thoroughly after transplanting.

- Keep the soil consistently moist throughout the growing season, especially during dry periods.
- Control weeds by mulching around onion plants and hand-weeding as necessary to prevent competition for nutrients and water.
- Onions have shallow root systems, so avoid deep cultivation that may damage the roots.

- Onions have moderate fertilizer requirements. Apply a balanced fertilizer (such as 10-10-10) at planting time and side-dress with nitrogen fertilizer (ammonium sulfate or calcium nitrate) every 3-4 weeks during the growing season.
- Avoid over-fertilization, as excessive nitrogen can promote leafy growth at the expense of bulb development.

- Onions are typically ready for harvest when the tops have turned yellow and started to topple over.
- Use a garden fork or shovel to loosen the soil around the bulbs, being careful not to damage them.
- Lift the bulbs gently from the soil and lay them out to dry in a warm, well-ventilated area for 1-2 weeks.
- Once the outer skins are dry and papery, trim the tops and roots, and store the onions in a cool, dry place with good air circulation.
